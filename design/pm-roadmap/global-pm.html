Most PM roadmaps online are wishlists, not plans.

I've worked alongside PMs for 20+ years — at startups, at scale, hiring them, firing them, mentoring them. So before we start, three honest things you must internalize:

One — Product Management is not a junior role at most serious companies. Google, Apple, Netflix almost never hire fresh grads as PMs. You will likely need 2–4 years of adjacent experience first (SWE, data, BA, or APM program). Plan accordingly.

Two — Your CSE degree is an asset, not a liability. Technical PMs are in higher demand than ever, especially in the AI era. Don't run from your engineering background — weaponize it.

Three — AI has not killed the PM role. It has killed the mediocre PM role. Generic feature-shippers are being automated; PMs with sharp judgment, taste, and customer empathy are more valuable than ever. Aim for the second category.

This roadmap is sequenced. Don't skip ahead. Each phase builds the foundation for the next, and shortcuts in early phases produce confident-but-shallow PMs — which is exactly the kind being squeezed out of the market right now.

01

Foundations — what product actually means.

Months 0 – 2 · Outcome: PM literacy
Time 8 weeks
Node 1.1

Understand what a PM actually does (and doesn't)

Why this matters
90% of CSE grads who say "I want to be a PM" are romanticizing the job. The reality: 30% strategy, 30% communication, 30% saying no, 10% glamour. Get this clear before investing 2 years.
Skills — what to learn
  • The PM role spectrum: APM, PM, Senior PM, Group PM, Director — what each does
  • PM vs Project Manager vs Program Manager — most beginners confuse all three
  • The product trio: PM + Designer + Engineer — how they collaborate
  • Outcomes vs outputs — the most important mental model in PM
Hands-on tasks
  • Pick 5 apps you use daily (bKash, Pathao, Foodpanda, YouTube, Notion). Write a 1-page note on what the PM there is likely optimizing for, and what trade-offs they're making
  • Shadow a PM in Bangladesh on LinkedIn — read every post they've written for the last 6 months
Free resources
  • Lenny's Newsletter — read the "PM Fundamentals" archive
  • YouTube: "Product School" channel — start with their "Day in the Life of a PM" playlist
  • Book (free PDF online): "Inspired" by Marty Cagan — chapters 1–6
  • Reforge blog (free articles) — search "what is a PM"
then →
Node 1.2

Master the core PM mental models

Why this matters
PMs are paid for judgment, not hours. Judgment comes from mental models. If you can't articulate "Jobs to be Done" in 30 seconds, you're not ready to call yourself a PM.
Skills
  • Jobs to be Done (JTBD) — Christensen's framework
  • The Kano Model — for prioritizing features
  • RICE, MoSCoW, ICE — prioritization frameworks (know all three)
  • The "5 Whys" and root cause analysis
  • Opportunity Solution Trees (Teresa Torres' model)
Hands-on tasks
  • Take Pathao Ride. Write a JTBD statement: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]." Defend it
  • Apply RICE to 5 features you'd add to bKash. Score them honestly
  • Build an Opportunity Solution Tree for "increase Foodpanda customer retention"
Free resources
  • Teresa Torres — "Continuous Discovery Habits" blog (free articles)
  • YouTube: Clayton Christensen "Milkshake Marketing" lecture
  • productplan.com — free framework guides

Discovery & customer truth — the art of asking.

Months 2 – 5 · Outcome: First user research artifact
Time 12 weeks
Node 2.1

User research & customer interviews

Why this matters
CSE grads suck at this. We're trained to think users behave logically. They don't. The PM's superpower is sitting with five strangers and extracting truth they didn't know they had.
Skills
  • The Mom Test — how to ask questions that don't get you lied to
  • Open vs leading questions
  • User personas & empathy maps
  • Synthesizing qualitative data (affinity diagrams)
Hands-on tasks
  • Critical project: Conduct 10 real user interviews. Pick a problem (e.g., "How do UIU students find tuition jobs?"). Interview students. Record. Synthesize
  • Write 3 user personas based on those interviews
  • Build an empathy map for your top persona
Free resources
  • Book (cheap/free PDF): "The Mom Test" by Rob Fitzpatrick — read it twice
  • Coursera: "User Research at Scale" — audit free
  • YouTube: "Continuous Discovery Habits" by Teresa Torres — full talk

Node 2.2

Market & competitive analysis

Why this matters
Your product doesn't exist in a vacuum. If you can't articulate why your product wins (or why it shouldn't exist), you can't make a single good decision about it.
Skills
  • SWOT, Porter's 5 Forces — the basics
  • TAM/SAM/SOM — market sizing for Bangladesh context
  • Competitive teardowns — feature, pricing, positioning
  • Reading 10-Ks & investor reports for public companies
Hands-on tasks
  • Pick a category (e.g., "ride-hailing in Dhaka"). Build a teardown comparing Pathao, Uber, Obhai across UX, pricing, driver supply
  • Estimate the TAM for digital tutoring in Bangladesh. Show your math. Defend it
Free resources
  • Stratechery (some articles free) — Ben Thompson

Execution craft — turning ideas into shipped product.

Months 5 – 9 · Outcome: A real product spec, end-to-end

Node 3.1

Writing PRDs, specs & user stories

Why this matters
A PM's writing is the product, before it exists. Sloppy specs produce sloppy products. This skill alone separates good PMs from average ones.
Skills
  • PRD structure: problem, users, goals, success metrics, scope, non-goals
  • User stories: "As a [user], I want [action], so that [outcome]"
  • Acceptance criteria & edge cases
  • Amazon's "Working Backwards" / press release method
Hands-on tasks
  • Write 3 full PRDs for features in apps you use. Be brutal. Include trade-offs. Include what you're NOT building and why
  • Write a "Press Release from the Future" for a new product. Pretend it launched. Make the customer the hero
Free resources
  • Lenny's Newsletter — "How the best PMs write PRDs" archive

Node 3.2

Agile, Scrum & working with engineering

Why this matters
As a CSE grad, you have an unfair advantage here. Most PMs fake technical fluency. You won't have to. But you still need to learn the rituals — sprints, standups, backlog grooming.

Hands-on tasks
  • Create a free Jira account. Set up a project. Run a fake 2-week sprint with 15 tickets across epics. Move them through the workflow
  • Volunteer to be the "PM" on a UIU friend's side project. Run their sprints for 4 weeks

Node 3.3

Design fluency & prototyping

Why this matters
You don't need to be a designer. You need to be conversant. You need to draw a wireframe, give meaningful design feedback, and not say "make it pop."
Skills

  • Wireframing (low-fi → high-fi)
  • Information architecture & user flows

Hands-on tasks

Free resources

Data, metrics & the quantitative PM.

Months 9 – 13 · Outcome: SQL fluency + a metrics framework
Time 16 weeks
Node 4.1

Product metrics & analytical thinking

Why this matters
"Vibes-based" PMs are getting fired in 2026. Every decision needs a metric. Every metric needs a sanity check. If you can't define your North Star, you don't have a product, you have a hobby.
Skills
  • North Star Metrics & input metrics
  • AARRR (Pirate Metrics): Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue
  • Cohort analysis & retention curves
  • Funnel analysis & drop-off diagnosis
  • Leading vs lagging indicators
Hands-on tasks
  • Pick a product. Define its North Star, 3 input metrics, and 3 guardrail metrics. Write a 1-pager defending each
  • Take a public retention curve (e.g., from Mixpanel benchmarks) and write a diagnosis: what's working, what's not
Free resources
  • Reforge — "Retention Engagement Growth" free articles
  • Amplitude's "North Star Playbook" — free PDF
  • Andrew Chen's blog — full archive on growth metrics
then →
Node 4.2

SQL & analytics tools

Why this matters
In 2026, a PM who can't write SQL is a liability. You'll be blocked on analysts for every basic question. SQL is the new Excel. Non-negotiable. Your CSE background makes this a weekend, not a semester.
Skills
  • SQL: SELECT, JOIN, GROUP BY, window functions, CTEs
  • Mixpanel / Amplitude — funnel & retention analysis
  • Google Analytics 4 — bare minimum literacy
  • A/B testing fundamentals: sample size, statistical significance, p-values, novelty effects
  • Excel/Sheets — pivot tables, VLOOKUP, basic forecasting
Hands-on tasks
  • Complete Mode Analytics SQL Tutorial end-to-end (free)
  • Take a public dataset (Kaggle e-commerce data). Answer 10 product questions in SQL
  • Set up a free Amplitude or Mixpanel demo and run 3 funnel analyses
  • Design an A/B test (with hypothesis, metric, sample size) on paper
Free resources

05

The AI-era PM — your unfair edge.

Months 13 – 18 · Outcome: Ship an AI-powered side project
Time 20 weeks
Node 5.1

How AI is reshaping the PM role

Why this matters
Honest take from 20 years in: AI is automating away the "execution-only" PM. Writing tickets, summarizing meetings, drafting specs — Claude and ChatGPT do that in seconds now. What's NOT being automated: judgment, taste, customer empathy, prioritization under ambiguity, and the ability to lead humans. Bend your career toward those.
Skills
  • Using AI as a thought partner — prompting Claude/ChatGPT for spec drafts, competitive analysis, user research synthesis, edge case generation
  • Knowing when NOT to trust AI — hallucinations, stale data, sycophancy
  • AI-augmented PM workflows: meeting summaries (Otter, Granola), customer feedback synthesis (Dovetail AI), spec drafts
  • Doubling your output — the goal is 2x leverage, not replacement
Hands-on tasks
  • Take 50 customer interview transcripts (or generate them). Use Claude/ChatGPT to synthesize themes. Compare to your manual synthesis. Note where AI was wrong
  • Use AI to generate 3 PRDs. Then critique them — what did AI miss that a human PM would catch?
  • Build an "AI cheat sheet" of 20 prompts you'd use weekly as a PM
Free resources
  • Anthropic's prompt engineering docs — free, excellent
  • Lenny + AI episodes (podcast & newsletter) — search "AI PM"
  • Aakash Gupta's "AI PM" newsletter
  • OpenAI Cookbook — even if you're not building, browse it
then →
Node 5.2

Building AI products (the new core skill)

Why this matters
Every serious product team in 2026 is shipping AI features. PMs who understand LLMs, evals, and AI UX patterns are commanding 30–50% premiums. This is THE differentiator for the next 5 years. Don't sleep on it.
Skills
  • LLM fundamentals: tokens, context windows, temperature, system prompts
  • RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — when to use it, when not to
  • Evals — how do you measure if an AI feature is "good"? This is the new A/B test
  • AI UX patterns: chat, autocomplete, agents, streaming, citations, fallbacks
  • Cost & latency trade-offs — model selection, caching, batching
  • Trust & safety: hallucinations, prompt injection, abuse
Hands-on tasks (this is critical)
  • Build & ship one real AI product. Use Claude API or ChatGPT API. Solve a Bangladesh-specific problem (e.g., Bangla → English document translator for SMEs, AI tuition matcher, agriculture chatbot for farmers). Use no-code tools (Bubble, Lovable, v0) if you must — but ship it
  • Write a teardown of one major AI product (Notion AI, Cursor, Perplexity). Cover: model choice, UX patterns, eval strategy, what they got right & wrong
  • Design an eval harness for your AI product — 20 test cases, scoring rubric
Free resources
  • Anthropic's "Building with Claude" docs — start here
  • Andrej Karpathy's "Intro to LLMs" YouTube talk — watch twice
  • Hamel Husain's blog on evals — gold standard
  • "AI Engineer" YouTube channel — conference talks

Section IV — Building Proof

Your portfolio.
Or: how strangers hire you.

The 5-piece PM portfolio

  1. Product teardown. Pick a product. Analyze it. Propose 3 improvements with metrics. 1500 words + visuals
  2. End-to-end case study. Real or fictional problem. Discovery → research → PRD → wireframes → metrics → launch plan. Your magnum opus
  3. Live AI product. Something you actually built and shipped. Even 50 users counts. URL + writeup
  4. Data-driven analysis. SQL + charts answering a real product question. Show your reasoning
  5. Strategy memo. 6-pager Amazon-style. Pick a Bangladeshi company. Tell them where to go next 3 years

Where to host it

  • Notion site or personal website — clean, fast, scannable. Don't over-design
  • Medium / Substack — write 1 post/month. Cross-post to LinkedIn
  • GitHub — for AI products and SQL work. Recruiters check
  • LinkedIn — your portfolio in public. Post weekly. This compounds
  • Loom walkthroughs — record a 3-min video for each case study. Recruiters love this

Portfolio rules

  • Show your thinking, not just outputs. Trade-offs you considered, why you rejected them
  • Quantify everything. "Increased retention" is dead. "Increased D7 retention from 22% to 31% over 6 weeks" gets calls
  • Pick problems Bangladeshi recruiters & global ones BOTH care about
  • Update it every quarter. A stale portfolio is a dead portfolio
Section V — Earning the Title

Interviewing for PM roles.

PM interviews are a different beast. Most CSE grads bomb them not because they're not smart, but because they answer like engineers. Here's the structure of what you'll face, and the frameworks that actually work.

Round Type 1

Product Sense / Product Design

"How would you improve YouTube?" "Design an app for elderly people in Dhaka." Tests: customer empathy, structured thinking, creativity, prioritization.

CIRCLES: Comprehend · Identify user · Report needs · Cut · List solutions · Evaluate · Summarize
Round Type 2

Analytical / Metrics

"YouTube views are down 5% — diagnose." Tests: structured problem-solving, metric fluency, comfort with ambiguity. Always segment, segment, segment.

Internal vs external · Then segment by user, geo, time, platform
Round Type 3

Strategy / Vision

"Should Netflix enter gaming?" Tests: strategic thinking, market understanding, ability to defend a position with data. Have an opinion, then back it.

Market → Capability → Customer → Competition → Recommendation
Round Type 4

Behavioral / Leadership

"Tell me about a conflict with an engineer." Tests: self-awareness, leadership, communication. Have 8–10 stories ready, mapped to Amazon's leadership principles.

STAR: Situation · Task · Action · Result (+ Learning)
Round Type 5

Technical / Estimation

Easier for you as a CSE grad. "Estimate Dhaka's daily ride-hailing volume." Show your math out loud. Make assumptions explicit. They want reasoning, not the right number.

Top-down · Bottom-up · Sanity check both ways
Round Type 6

Execution / Trade-offs

"Engineering says it'll take 6 months. PR launch is in 3. What do you do?" Tests: how you handle reality. There's no right answer — there's a defensible answer.

Constraints → Options → Trade-offs → Decision → Communicate
How to actually prepare
Do 50 mock interviews. Yes, fifty. With friends, with strangers on Exponent or IGotAnOffer, with paid coaches if you can afford it. Record them. Watch yourself. You'll cringe. That's the point.
Free resources → Exponent (free question bank) · "Decode and Conquer" by Lewis Lin · Lenny's interview prep archive · YouTube: "Diana Yu" PM interview channel
Section VI — Things People Won't Tell You

Six uncomfortable truths.

i

Your first PM job will probably not be as a PM.

Realistically, in Bangladesh, you'll start as: a Software Engineer (1–2 yrs) → Associate PM or Product Analyst → PM. Or: Business Analyst → PM. Or: founder of a side project that gets traction. APM programs at Google/Microsoft/Atlassian exist, but accept <1% of applicants. Plan the long route, get pleasantly surprised by the short one.

ii

Bangladesh's PM market is small. Use that.

Top PM employers locally: bKash, Pathao, Chaldal, ShopUp, Kludio, 10 Minute School, Daraz, Shohoz. Reach out directly. The market is small enough that LinkedIn DMs to PMs there actually get replies. This is your unfair advantage over candidates from larger markets.

iii

Google, Apple, Netflix won't hire you straight from UIU. That's fine.

The realistic path to a top MNC PM role: 2–3 years at a respected company (locally or remote-first global startup) → MBA at a top program OR 1–2 years at a Series B+ startup with global recognition → MNC. Or: build something that gets noticed, get acqui-hired. Both work.

iv

Building your own product is harder than getting a PM job. By a lot.

If your goal is a Bangladeshi product in 5 years, working as a PM at a fast-growing local company first is almost always the right move. You'll learn, network, save capital, and validate ideas. The "drop out and build" narrative is survivorship bias. Most successful Bangladeshi founders worked somewhere first.

v

Your network beats your resume. Always has.

80% of PM roles at the senior level are filled through networks, not job boards. Start now. Show up at Dhaka product meetups. DM people. Comment thoughtfully on LinkedIn posts. Help others before asking for help. In 24 months, your network is your career. Not a metaphor — literally.

vi

The PMs who stay relevant in 10 years aren't the smartest. They're the most curious.

The product world reinvents itself every 3 years. Mobile. SaaS. Crypto. AI. The next thing. PMs who grew up loving one paradigm and refused to learn the next are gone. Make curiosity your operating system. Read outside your domain. Ship things. Be wrong publicly. The rest follows.

Section VII — Choosing Your Lane

Two five-year plans.
Pick one. For now.

Path A · MNC Product Manager

The credential path. Optimized for Google, Apple, Netflix.

Year 1
Software Engineer at a Bangladeshi startup with global ambitions (ShopUp, Tiger New Energy, etc.). Build technical credibility. Volunteer for product-adjacent work.
Year 2
Transition internally to APM or Product Analyst. Ship 2 features that move metrics. Build portfolio in parallel.
Year 3
Apply to APM programs at global remote-first companies. Or land a PM role at a Series B+ startup with US/EU presence. Move there if needed.
Year 4
MBA at a top-15 program (INSEAD, Wharton, Stanford GSB) — IF you can self-fund or get scholarships. This becomes your bridge to FAANG. Or: deepen at the global startup.
Year 5
Apply to Google/Meta/Netflix PM roles. Use the MBA network or startup brand. Realistically: 100+ applications, 3–5 onsites, 1 offer if you've executed well.
Path B · Founder in Bangladesh

The builder path. Optimized for shipping your own product.

Year 1
SWE or PM at a Bangladeshi product company. Save aggressively. Build side projects every weekend. Start collecting "problems worth solving" in a Notion doc.
Year 2
Move into a PM role. Get exposure to: monetization, ops, growth, fundraising (sit in on board meetings if possible). Validate 2–3 startup ideas in nights/weekends.
Year 3
Pick ONE idea with real signal (paying customers, not just sign-ups). Build a co-founding team — ideally one strong engineer + one strong sales/ops person. Quit when you have 6 months runway and 10 paying customers.
Year 4
Full-time on the product. Apply to Y Combinator, Antler, or local accelerators. Raise a pre-seed if needed. Get to ৳1 crore ARR or product-market fit signals.
Year 5
Scale or pivot. Either you've built a real business serving Bangladeshi customers, or you've learned more in 2 years than most PMs learn in 10. Both are wins.

The honest truth: you don't have to choose today. Phases 1–4 are identical for both paths. The fork happens around month 18. By then, you'll know yourself well enough to choose with conviction.
