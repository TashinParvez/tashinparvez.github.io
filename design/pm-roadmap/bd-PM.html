Node 1.1 Priority · Critical

What a PM actually does at a Bangladeshi company

Why: The PM role in BD is NOT the Silicon Valley PM role. At bKash, you'll do more stakeholder management with banks. At Pathao, more ops coordination. At 10MS, more content alignment. Get this clear before you waste 6 months prepping for the wrong job.
Mini-lesson · The BD PM reality

A PM at a Dhaka company spends roughly: 40% stakeholder alignment (compliance, business, ops, sometimes regulators), 25% writing & specs, 20% data & analysis, 10% user research, 5% strategy.

Compare to a Google PM: 30% strategy, 25% data, 20% writing, 15% stakeholder, 10% research. The difference: BD PMs operate in resource-constrained, regulation-heavy, mobile-first markets. Your job is to get things shipped despite friction, not to dream up moonshots.

Skills — what to learn
  • The role spectrum at BD companies: Product Analyst → APM → PM → Senior PM → Lead PM
  • How "Product Manager" titles in BD often blur with Project Manager, Business Analyst, Product Owner — and how to read JDs accurately
  • Reading bdjobs.com PM listings and decoding what they actually want
  • The bKash / Pathao / 10MS reporting structures (who PMs report to, who they fight with)
Hands-on tasks
  • Read 20 PM job postings on bdjobs.com & LinkedIn. Build a spreadsheet of common requirements. The patterns are eye-opening
  • Find 5 PMs working at the target companies on LinkedIn. Read every post they've published in the last 12 months
  • Write a 1-page note: "What does a PM at bKash do that a PM at 10MS doesn't?" Defend with evidence

Node 1.2 Priority · High

Core frameworks every PM uses (condensed)

Why: Frameworks aren't ceremony. They're the shared vocabulary of product. When your interviewer says "RICE this for me," you can't blink. Learn the 6 below cold. Skip the rest until you need them.
Mini-lesson · The 6 frameworks you must know

1. JTBD (Jobs to Be Done): Customers don't buy products, they "hire" them to do a job. When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]

2. RICE prioritization: Score features by Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. RICE = (R × I × C) / E

3. AARRR (Pirate Metrics): Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral → Revenue. The funnel every consumer product lives in.

4. North Star Metric: ONE metric that captures the value your product delivers. For bKash: monthly transacting users. For 10MS: minutes-watched-per-active-user.

5. The Kano Model: Features fall into 3 buckets — basic (must-haves), performance (more = better), delighters (surprises). Build basic first, always.

6. 5 Whys: When a metric drops, ask "why" five times. The first answer is never the real cause. The fifth usually is.

Skills
  • JTBD framework — apply to bKash send-money, Pathao Food, Shikho subscription
  • Prioritization: RICE, MoSCoW, ICE, Value vs Effort matrix
  • Pirate Metrics & funnel diagnosis
  • North Star + input/guardrail metrics
Hands-on tasks
  • Write JTBD statements for 3 BD products. Make them specific — "When I'm at the kacha bazar with no cash, I want to pay the vendor without showing my balance to others, so I don't get pressured to give more"
  • Score 6 Pathao Food feature ideas using RICE. Be honest about your Confidence numbers
  • Define a North Star + 3 input metrics for 10 Minute School. Defend each in 50 words
Free resources

  • Amplitude's North Star Playbook — free PDF
  • YouTube: "Clayton Christensen Milkshake Marketing" — 7 min, watch twice
  • Reforge free articles on retention
Node 1.3 Priority · High · Portfolio

Product teardowns of BD apps — your first portfolio piece

Why: Recruiters at bKash and Pathao read teardowns of their own products with intense interest. A well-written teardown of bKash's send-money flow gets you a callback. A generic teardown of Spotify does not.
Mini-lesson · How to do a teardown that gets noticed

A good teardown has 4 parts: (1) The problem — what is this product trying to solve? (2) The current solution — annotated screenshots showing what works and what doesn't. (3) The metrics — what would you, the PM, be measuring? (4) Three improvements — with hypothesized impact, prioritized by RICE.

The mistake everyone makes: redesigning the UI. Don't. PMs aren't designers. Show product thinking, not visual taste. A 1500-word doc with smart hypotheses beats a beautiful Figma file every time.

Tasks (do all three)
  • Teardown 1 — bKash send-money flow: Why is the PIN entered last? What's the activation rate of new users sending money for the first time? Three improvements
  • Teardown 2 — Pathao Food restaurant search: Why does it surface "popular near you" first? What metric does this optimize? Three improvements
  • Teardown 3 — 10MS course completion: Why do most users drop off in chapter 3? What's the retention curve? Three interventions
Format
  • 1500 words each, posted publicly on Notion or Medium
  • Include 3-5 annotated screenshots
  • End with: "If I were the PM, the top thing I'd ship next quarter is X, because Y"
  • Tag the company on LinkedIn when you post. PMs there will see it
Resources
  • Search "Lenny Rachitsky teardown template" — use his structure
  • Read Sachin Rekhi's teardown of LinkedIn — gold standard
  • Look up "GoodDay" teardowns archive — many BD-product teardowns by PMs

Discovery, research & customer truth.

Months 2 – 5 · Outcome: 10 user interviews + 1 research artifact
Duration12 wks
Node 2.1 Priority · Critical

User research — and why CSE grads are bad at it

Why: Engineers think users are rational. They aren't. A bKash user in Tongi will tell you the app is "fine" — and then mention in passing that her husband has to help her every time she sends money. That's the real signal. Learn to hear it.
Mini-lesson · The 4 rules of user interviews (The Mom Test)

Rule 1: Talk about their life, not your idea. "How do you currently send money to your family in the village?" beats "Would you like a faster send-money feature?"

Rule 2: Ask about specifics in the past, not opinions about the future. "Walk me through the last time you sent money" beats "How often do you send money?"

Rule 3: Talk less, listen more. 80/20 rule. They talk 80% of the time. Most beginners do the opposite.

Rule 4: Watch for pain, workarounds, and money spent. If someone has built a workaround or paid money to solve something, that's a real problem. Everything else is polite chatter.

Skills
  • Interview design — open vs leading questions
  • Affinity mapping — synthesizing 10+ interviews into themes
  • Personas & empathy maps — based on real interviews, not stereotypes
  • Bangla-language interviewing — most BD users open up only in Bangla. Practice both
Hands-on tasks (the most important task in this entire roadmap)
  • Run 10 real user interviews. Pick a problem your community knows: "How UIU students find tuition jobs" or "How small Dhaka shopkeepers manage daily cash". Interview 10 people. Record (with permission)
  • Synthesize into 3 themes, 2 personas, 1 empathy map
  • Write a 5-page research report with quotes, themes, and a "what to build" recommendation
  • Post on LinkedIn. Tag relevant companies
Free resources

  • Teresa Torres "Continuous Discovery" — full free YouTube talks
  • NN/g articles — Nielsen Norman Group, free, gold standard

Node 2.2 Priority · Medium

BD market & competitive analysis

Why: If you walk into a Pathao interview and don't know who Uber, Obhai, and Shohoz Rides are doing differently, you're done. Domain knowledge is leverage. It also makes your interview answers 10x sharper.
Mini-lesson · TAM/SAM/SOM for BD products

TAM (Total Addressable Market): Everyone who could conceivably use this. SAM (Serviceable): Those you can realistically reach. SOM (Obtainable): Those you'll actually capture.

Example for digital tutoring in BD: TAM = ~40M school-age kids. SAM = ~12M with smartphone access in households earning >৳25K/month. SOM = ~2M reachable through digital marketing in 3 years.

Always show your work. Always sanity-check both top-down (industry reports) and bottom-up (per-user × users). When they agree within 30%, you're solid.

Skills

  • BD market sizing — TAM/SAM/SOM with Bangladesh data
  • Competitive teardowns — feature, pricing, distribution
  • Reading BD industry reports — LightCastle, BIDA, Bangladesh Bank publications
Hands-on tasks
  • Build a competitive matrix: Pathao vs Uber vs Obhai vs Shohoz across pricing, driver supply, geography, brand, app UX
  • Estimate TAM/SAM/SOM for "1-on-1 online tutoring in Bangladesh." Show all assumptions
  • Write a 1-pager: "Why bKash beats Nagad" — back with data, not opinions

Execution craft — the shipping machine.

Months 5 – 9 · Outcome: 3 PRDs + Jira fluency + Figma comfort
Duration16 wks
Node 3.1 Priority · Critical

Writing PRDs that engineers actually read

Why: bKash's JD literally says "Converting product features into meaningful requirements, user stories, and acceptance criteria." That IS the PRD. Bad PRDs cause shipped bugs. Bad shipped bugs cost you the trust of your engineering team forever.
Mini-lesson · The 7-section PRD that works in BD

1. Problem — One paragraph. The customer pain in plain language.

2. Users & use cases — Who, when, where, why. Specific, not generic.

3. Goals & non-goals — What success looks like. What we are explicitly NOT solving.

4. Success metrics — North star + 2 input metrics + 1 guardrail.

5. User stories & acceptance criteria — As a [user], I want to [action], so I can [outcome] — plus testable acceptance criteria for each.

6. Edge cases & failure modes — What if the network drops? What if the user is on a feature phone? Engineers will love you for this.

7. Open questions & risks — Be honest. PRDs without risks are PRDs that haven't been thought through.

Skills
  • Writing user stories with proper acceptance criteria
  • Edge case generation — your CSE background is your superpower here
  • Working Backwards / press-release style (Amazon's method)
  • Writing for Bangladeshi engineers — clear, unambiguous, well-translated terminology
Hands-on tasks (write 3 PRDs — these go in your portfolio)
  • PRD 1: "bKash NID-based account recovery for users without phone access"
  • PRD 2: "Pathao Food — group order for office colleagues"
  • PRD 3: "Shikho — offline mode for low-bandwidth students in district towns"
  • Get an engineer friend (real one) to read each. Iterate based on their questions

Node 3.2 Priority · Critical · BD-Specific

JIRA, Agile & Scrum — the BD office stack

Why: bKash's JD literally says "thorough understanding of JIRA." Pathao runs sprints. Almost every BD tech company uses Atlassian. Your CSE training already knows what an API is. Now learn how a sprint actually runs.
Mini-lesson · Scrum in 90 seconds

A sprint is a fixed time-box (usually 2 weeks) in which the team commits to shipping a defined set of work. Five rituals:

(1) Sprint planning (Monday morning, ~2 hrs) — team picks tickets from the backlog. (2) Daily standup (15 min, every morning) — what I did, what I'll do, what's blocking me. (3) Backlog grooming (mid-sprint, 1 hr) — refining future tickets. (4) Sprint review (Friday end, demos). (5) Retrospective (after review) — what worked, what didn't.

Story points use the Fibonacci sequence (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13). They measure complexity, not hours. A 1 is "trivial CSS tweak." A 13 is "I'm not sure we can do this in one sprint." T-shirt sizing (XS, S, M, L, XL) works the same way.

Skills
  • Scrum ceremonies — how to run each one without wasting time
  • JIRA mastery — epics, stories, sub-tasks, sprints, dashboards, JQL
  • Backlog grooming & estimation — story points, planning poker
  • Kanban — for ops/support teams; know when to use it over Scrum
  • Confluence — most BD companies pair it with JIRA for docs
Hands-on tasks
  • Create a free JIRA account. Set up a project. Build 3 epics and 20 stories. Run 2 fake sprints with proper status transitions
  • Get Atlassian's JIRA Fundamentals + Agile certifications — both free, both look great on a CV in BD
  • Volunteer to run sprints for one of your friend's CSE thesis projects. Real teams, real friction. 4 weeks minimum
Free resources

  • Scrum.org — free Scrum Guide, 50 pages, read it twice

Node 3.3 Priority · High

API literacy — the bKash-shaped skill

Why: bKash's JD lists "Expertise in APIs" as a hard requirement. Why? Because bKash is a platform — half the company's products live on top of merchant APIs, partner APIs, BRAC Bank APIs. A PM who can't read an API contract is dead weight. Your CSE background makes this easy.
Mini-lesson · APIs for PMs (the 80/20)

An API is a contract: "send me X in this shape, I'll return Y in that shape." For PMs, you don't need to write APIs. You need to read them, design what they should do, and trade off between options.

The 4 things you MUST know: (1) REST verbs — GET (read), POST (create), PUT (update), DELETE. (2) JSON structure — keys, values, nesting. (3) Status codes — 200 = success, 4xx = client's fault, 5xx = our fault. (4) Auth — API keys, OAuth, JWT (just the concepts).

Bigger PM concepts: idempotency (safe to retry?), rate limits (how many calls/sec?), versioning (v1 → v2 migration), webhooks (push vs pull). When you can sit in a meeting and say "if we make this idempotent, we won't double-charge customers on retries," you've earned engineering's respect.

Skills
  • REST API basics — verbs, status codes, JSON, headers
  • Postman — making and testing API calls without writing code
  • Reading API docs — Stripe, bKash Open API, Pathao Courier API
  • Concepts: idempotency, rate limiting, webhooks, OAuth flows
Hands-on tasks
  • Use Postman to call a public API (e.g., OpenWeather, GitHub). Make 10 different calls
  • Read the bKash Open API docs end-to-end. Write a 1-page summary of what their API exposes & what's missing
  • Design (in a doc) a new API endpoint for Pathao: "GET /drivers/nearby" — what params? what response? what edge cases?
Free resources
  • Postman Learning Center — free, structured

  • YouTube: "Web Dev Simplified" — REST API in 100 seconds

Node 3.4 Priority · Medium

Figma fluency — enough to be dangerous

Why: Don't try to be a designer. Be conversant. You need to wireframe a flow, give meaningful feedback ("the back button breaks user flow on screen 3" beats "make it pop"), and clickthrough prototypes in interviews.
Mini-lesson · Nielsen's 10 Heuristics (memorize these)

The 10 universal UX rules every PM should reference: (1) Visibility of system status (loaders, progress bars). (2) Match between system & real world (use words users know). (3) User control & freedom (undo, back). (4) Consistency & standards. (5) Error prevention.

(6) Recognition over recall (show options, don't make users remember). (7) Flexibility & efficiency (shortcuts for power users). (8) Aesthetic & minimalist design. (9) Help users recover from errors (clear messages, suggestions). (10) Help & documentation.

In a heuristic evaluation, walk through a flow and tag each violation. Apply this to bKash's send-money. You'll find at least 4 violations.

  • Wireframing low-fi → high-fi

  • Heuristic evaluation (Nielsen's 10)
Hands-on tasks

  • Run a heuristic evaluation on the bKash app. Document 10 issues with screenshots

Node 4.1 Priority · Critical · Pathao + bKash love this

Product metrics, retention curves & funnel analysis

Why: Pathao's APM JD literally says "Data-driven and able to tell what the data says and what it means, and use that to inform product direction." Every BD interview asks: "If your DAU drops 10%, what do you do?" If you don't have a structured answer, you're done.
Mini-lesson · How to diagnose a metric drop

When DAU drops 10%, never guess. Always segment in this order:

(1) Internal vs External: Did we ship something? Did a competitor launch? Holiday? Outage? (2) New vs Returning users: Acquisition problem or retention problem? (3) Geography: All of BD? Just Dhaka? Just Sylhet? (4) Platform: iOS? Android? Both? Particular Android version? (5) Cohort: Which signup month started dropping?

Most metric drops have ONE explanation. Your job is to keep slicing until you find it. The PMs who stand out in interviews don't jump to causes — they jump to where they'd look first.

Skills
  • North Star + input/guardrail metrics
  • AARRR funnel analysis & drop-off diagnosis
  • Cohort analysis & retention curves — D1, D7, D30 retention
  • Leading vs lagging indicators
  • The "L7" / "L28" engagement metric (days active in last 7/28)
Hands-on tasks
  • Pick a product (e.g., Shikho). Define North Star + 3 input + 2 guardrail metrics. Defend each in writing
  • Take a fictional dataset (or use Kaggle). Build a funnel chart in Sheets. Diagnose drop-off
  • Write a 1-pager: "Pathao Food orders dropped 15% this month — here's how I'd diagnose it" — practice this exact format. They WILL ask it

Node 4.2 Priority · Critical · Non-negotiable

SQL — the new Excel

Why: A PM who can't write SQL waits 3 days for a data analyst to answer "what was yesterday's signup conversion?" PMs who write their own queries answer it in 3 minutes. The compounding career advantage is enormous. Your CSE background makes this a weekend, not a semester.
Mini-lesson · The 6 SQL patterns every PM uses

(1) Aggregate counts: SELECT COUNT(*) FROM users WHERE signup_date = '2026-05-01';

(2) Group by: SELECT country, COUNT(*) FROM users GROUP BY country;

(3) Joins: Connecting two tables (users + transactions) on a shared key.

(4) Window functions: ROW_NUMBER, LAG, LEAD — for cohort analysis and "first X per user" queries.

(5) CTEs (WITH clause): Breaking complex queries into readable steps. Senior PMs write CTEs. Beginners write 200-line nested subqueries.

(6) Date functions: DATE_TRUNC, DATEDIFF, INTERVAL — for any time-based analysis. Most PM questions are time-based.

Skills
  • SQL basics: SELECT, WHERE, JOIN, GROUP BY, ORDER BY, LIMIT
  • Intermediate: CTEs, window functions, CASE WHEN, UNION
  • Advanced for PMs: retention queries, cohort tables, funnel queries
Hands-on tasks

Node 4.3 Priority · High

Analytics tools & A/B testing

Why: Pathao runs experiments constantly. 10MS measures everything. If you walk in not knowing what statistical significance means or how to design a test, you'll embarrass yourself.
Mini-lesson · A/B testing in 5 rules

(1) Hypothesis first: "Changing the CTA from 'Send' to 'Pay Now' will increase send-money completion rate by 5%, because 'Pay' is more familiar to merchant users."

(2) Sample size matters: Smaller effect = bigger sample. Use a calculator. Running an underpowered test is worse than not testing.

(3) Don't peek: Calling a test early because results look good = self-deception. Set duration up front, stick to it.

(4) Watch for novelty effect: New thing always performs better in week 1. Run for 2-4 weeks minimum.

(5) Always have a guardrail metric: CTA change might lift conversions but drop retention. Always check both.

Skills
  • Mixpanel / Amplitude — funnels, retention, cohorts

  • A/B testing — hypothesis, sample size, p-values, novelty effects
  • Excel/Sheets — pivot tables, VLOOKUP, basic forecasting
Hands-on tasks

AI fluency — your unfair edge in the BD market

Why: Most BD PM candidates in 2026 have weak AI literacy. This is your moat. A PM who can prototype a feature with Claude in an afternoon, design good evals, and reason about when AI fits — that PM is rare in Dhaka. Lean in.
Mini-lesson · The PM's mental model for LLMs

Think of an LLM as a very knowledgeable, very fast new employee with three quirks: (1) Sometimes confidently wrong (hallucination). (2) Forgets the conversation when you start a new chat (context window). (3) Can't access your data unless you give it (RAG).

The PM's job with AI features is to design around these quirks. Mitigations: citations (force it to ground answers), evals (test against known-good answers), guardrails (block bad outputs), human-in-loop (escalate uncertain cases), and graceful fallbacks (when AI fails, what happens?).

For BD specifically: think hard about Bangla language quality. Most LLMs are 5x better in English than Bangla. If your product serves Bangla users, this is a constraint that shapes your entire roadmap.

Skills
  • Using Claude/ChatGPT for PM workflows — spec drafts, research synthesis, edge cases
  • LLM fundamentals: tokens, context windows, temperature, system prompts
  • RAG, evals, prompt engineering — at conceptual depth
  • AI UX patterns: chat, autocomplete, agents, citations, streaming
  • Bangla NLP nuances — what works, what doesn't
Hands-on tasks

Why: The Bangladeshi job market runs on referrals. ~70% of PM hires at top BD companies come through internal referrals or warm intros. Cold-applying via bdjobs.com works, but is the slowest path. Network deliberately.
Mini-lesson · The 90-day BD networking playbook

Days 1-30 — Build presence. Post on LinkedIn weekly: a teardown, a mini-lesson, a take. Comment thoughtfully on 5 PM posts/week. Update your headline to "PM in training · CSE @ UIU · sharing what I learn."

Days 31-60 — Reach out. Find 30 PMs at target companies on LinkedIn. Send each a thoughtful message: "Hi [name], I read your post on X — your point about Y stuck with me. Fintech · Most process-heavy
What they really hire for

Process maturity, API/agile fluency, ability to navigate compliance. They're a regulated bank-adjacent entity. They want PMs who don't break things.

Hidden bar

You'll work with BRAC Bank stakeholders. Comfort in formal corporate environments matters more than at Pathao or 10MS.

Prepare with
  • 3 detailed teardowns of bKash flows (send, payment, savings)
  • API design exercise (their JD asks)
  • 1 case on regulatory edge: "How would you launch a feature that needs Bangladesh Bank approval?"
  • Read Bangladesh Bank's MFS regulations cover-to-cover
Likely entry path

Officer / Senior Officer in Product & Tech division. Often via internal SWE → product transition. APM not always titled.

Pathao
Logistics · Most data-heavy
What they really hire for

Data-driven decision making, marketplace thinking (supply-demand balance), comfort with ambiguity. CSE/EEE grads from MIST, BUET, BRAC, AUST predominate.

Hidden bar

They run experiments constantly. You should be able to talk about A/B tests in your sleep. Marketplace dynamics — driver supply economics — comes up in every interview.

Prepare with
  • SQL fluency — non-negotiable
  • 1 marketplace case study: "How would you increase Pathao Food driver retention?"
  • Teardowns of Pathao Food, Rides, Parcel
  • A/B test design walkthrough
Likely entry path

Product Analyst → APM → PM. Very common. They have a clear ladder.

Shikho
EdTech · Most startup-feel
What they really hire for

Generalists who can wear many hats. Mobile-first thinking. Subscription / freemium economics. Empathy for school-age users in district towns, not just Dhaka.

Hidden bar

Smaller team = higher leverage but higher chaos. Self-direction matters more than process. You'll ship faster but with less guidance.

Prepare with
  • EdTech retention frameworks (D7, D30, content engagement)
  • Case: "How would you double Shikho's free-to-paid conversion?"
  • Mobile-first UX teardown
  • Understanding of NCTB curriculum (the actual syllabus they teach against)
Likely entry path

Product Analyst or APM directly. Smaller team = faster path to PM title.

10 Minute School
EdTech · Most growth-obsessed
What they really hire for

Growth + content product fluency. They have the largest BD audience in EdTech. PMs here think a lot about content-product fit, course completion, viral mechanics.

Hidden bar

Brand-driven culture (Ayman Sadiq is the founder, public figure). Comfort with brand-product trade-offs matters.

Prepare with
  • Funnel diagnosis — they care about completion rates obsessively
  • Content-product hybrid thinking
  • Case: "Course completion is 35%. How do you raise it to 60%?"
  • Multi-format product knowledge (live, recorded, skill courses)
Likely entry path

Product Analyst or APM. Strong internal mobility.

BrainStation 23
Services · PM = BA/PM hybrid
What they really hire for

Be careful with the title. Many "PM" roles here are Business Analyst + Project Manager hybrid for client work. Less product strategy, more requirements + delivery.

Hidden bar

Strong written/verbal English (clients are international). Project delivery skills matter as much as product thinking.

Prepare with
  • Solid BA fundamentals — requirements gathering, BRD writing
  • JIRA + project tracking mastery
  • Client communication examples
  • Comfort with the difference between product PM vs services BA
Likely entry path

BA / Project Manager / Tech Lead first. Not a pure PM job, but a real foot in the door for fresh grads.

Enosis
Outsourced product · execution-first
What they really hire for

Builds for US clients. PM-adjacent roles include Tech Lead, BA, sometimes Product Owner. Excellent shop for learning craft & quality at scale.

Hidden bar

Time-zone overlap with US is mandatory. Strong remote-collaboration habits valued.

Prepare with
  • Strong CSE engineering credibility — they value technical depth
  • Solid English communication (mostly written)
  • Examples of taking initiative on projects
Likely entry path

SDE → Tech Lead → adjacent PM/PO role. Long path to a pure PM title, but you'll learn an enormous amount.

Section IV · The Extra Skills

What separates good PMs from average ones in BD.

These don't appear on JDs but show up in every interview, every promotion conversation, every coffee with a senior PM. Build them in parallel with the technical roadmap.

i

Bilingual fluency, both directions

You'll write specs in English (engineers, leadership) but interview users in Bangla. Translating user pain across the two languages without losing nuance is a real skill.

→ Run 5 user interviews in Bangla. Synthesize in English. Notice what gets lost.
ii

Influence without authority

You're a fresh grad with no formal power telling 30-year-old engineers what to build. They don't have to listen to you. Earning their respect is a skill — clarity, preparation, admitting when you don't know.

→ Read "Influence" by Cialdini. Practice in your CSE group projects.
iii

Saying no — and saying it well

Senior PMs say no 10x more than they say yes. The skill isn't refusing — it's refusing without burning the relationship. "Not now, here's why" beats "no" every time.

→ Practice writing 5 polite rejections to feature requests. Get feedback.
iv

Written communication craft

PMs are paid to write. Memos, status updates, proposals, post-mortems. Being a clear writer compounds — every doc you write becomes more persuasive than the last.

→ Write 1 LinkedIn post per week for 6 months. Public writing forces clarity.
v

The "managing up" muscle

Your CEO, your VP, your engineering lead — each needs different things from you. Senior PMs intuitively adapt their updates per audience. Juniors send everyone the same email.

→ Read First Round Review's "managing up" archive. It's free.
vi

Domain depth in ONE area

Generalist PMs are commodities. Specialist PMs are scarce. Pick a vertical (fintech, edtech, logistics, AI) and go deep. After 2 years, you'll be the go-to person on that domain in BD.

→ Pick fintech OR edtech now. Read 1 industry report/week. Compounds fast.
vii

Comfort with ambiguity

Engineers are paid for correct answers. PMs are paid for good decisions under uncertainty. The discomfort never goes away — but you stop fighting it.

→ When a problem feels ambiguous, write 3 plausible answers. Pick one. Defend it. Move on.
viii

Conflict resolution

Engineering wants quality. Sales wants speed. Design wants polish. You're in the middle. The PM who runs from conflict gets steamrolled. The one who facilitates it well gets promoted.

→ Read "Crucial Conversations." Apply at your next project disagreement.
ix

Customer empathy as a daily habit

Most PMs claim it. Few practice it. Spend 1 hour/week using your product as a customer would. Sit in a customer support call. Read 20 reviews. Notice what frustrates real people, not personas.

→ Block 1 hour every Friday on your calendar: "Be a user."
Section V · The Interview Loop

How BD interviews actually run.

The BD PM interview is shorter than Google's (2-4 rounds vs 6-8) but tests the same fundamentals. Here's the structure, what each round really evaluates, and the framework that works.

i
Round 1 · Resume Screen

30 minutes · HR or junior PM

Tests: communication clarity, why-product motivation, basic role understanding. Surface-level. Get this right by being specific and confident, not by being clever.

The 3 questions you WILL get: "Why product?" · "Why us?" · "Tell me about yourself."

Tip: Have a 60-second story. CSE journey → why you got curious about product → 1 specific thing you've shipped or analyzed → why this company specifically. Practice it 50 times.
ii
Round 2 · Product Sense

45-60 minutes · Senior PM

"How would you improve bKash?" "Design a feature for shopkeepers in Old Dhaka." Tests: customer empathy, structured thinking, prioritization, creativity. The single highest-signal round.

Use a structured framework. Don't wing it.

CIRCLES Framework:
C — Comprehend the situation
I — Identify the user
R — Report user needs
C — Cut through prioritization
L — List solutions
E — Evaluate trade-offs
S — Summarize recommendation
iii
Round 3 · Analytical / Metrics

45 minutes · PM lead or analyst

"Pathao Food orders dropped 12% last week. Diagnose." Tests: structured problem-solving, metric fluency, comfort with ambiguity. Always segment, segment, segment.

Diagnosis flow they want to see:

Internal vs External →
New vs Returning users →
Geography (Dhaka? Sylhet?) →
Platform (iOS? Android?) →
Cohort segmentation →
Hypothesis → Validation plan
iv
Round 4 · Technical / Execution

45 minutes · Engineering lead or staff PM

For CSE grads, this is your strength. They'll test API knowledge, edge case thinking, sprint trade-offs. "Engineering says it'll take 6 months but launch is in 3. What do you do?"

Trade-off framework they want:

Constraints (real & flexible) →
Options (cut scope / extend / add resources / launch staged) →
Trade-offs explicit (cost, quality, timeline) →
Decision with reasoning →
Communication plan to stakeholders
v
Round 5 · Behavioral & Culture

30-45 minutes · Hiring manager / Director

"Tell me about a conflict." "Tell me about a failure." Tests: self-awareness, leadership, fit. BD companies care a LOT about cultural alignment — humility, ownership, team-orientation.

Have 8-10 stories ready, mapped to common themes (conflict, failure, leadership, ambiguity, customer focus, learning). Use STAR.

STAR + L:
S — Situation (context)
T — Task (your role)
A — Action (what YOU did)
R — Result (numbers if possible)
L — Learning (what would you do differently)
